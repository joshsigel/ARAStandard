# Executive Summary
The Autonomous Reliability Assurance Foundation (ARAF) operates a multi-stakeholder ecosystem for the certification, continuous monitoring, and insurance of autonomous systems. This document defines the six participant categories within the ARAF ecosystem, their roles, rights, obligations, accreditation requirements, and governance relationships.
Unlike traditional certification frameworks that rely on point-in-time audits, the ARA Standard is designed around persistent certification state — continuous monitoring that ensures autonomous systems remain in compliance throughout their operational lifecycle, not just at the moment of evaluation. # Foreword
The ARA Standard exists to answer a simple question for organizations deploying autonomous systems: Is this system operationally reliable enough to trust with autonomous action in production?
Answering that question requires more than a standard published by a single body. It requires an ecosystem: evaluators who can assess compliance, platforms that can monitor compliance continuously, insurers who can underwrite the risk, and a governance structure that ensures the standard evolves with the technology it governs.
This document defines that ecosystem. It establishes the roles, requirements, and relationships for every participant category, from the Technical Standards Board that maintains the standard to the certified organizations that deploy under it. It is designed to be the authoritative reference for anyone seeking to understand how the ARAF ecosystem works and how to participate in it.
A key architectural distinction separates ARAF from legacy certification frameworks. Traditional standards like SOC 2 or ISO 27001 rely on annual point-in-time audits. An auditor visits, reviews evidence, issues a report, and leaves. For the next twelve months, compliance is assumed. This model is fundamentally inadequate for autonomous systems, which can drift, degrade, or develop new failure modes between audits. The ARA Standard addresses this through persistent certification state: continuous telemetry-based monitoring that verifies compliance in real-time, every hour of every day.
This architectural choice creates a participant category that does not exist in legacy ecosystems: the Continuous Assurance Platform Operator (CAPO). CAPOs are technology platforms accredited by ARAF to ingest telemetry from certified systems, run real-time compliance checks, trigger revalidation alerts, and feed risk signals to insurers. They are the infrastructure layer that makes persistent certification possible.
The separation of evaluation (AVBs) from monitoring (CAPOs) is intentional. It creates a marketplace dynamic where AVBs can partner with any accredited CAPO, CAPOs can serve systems evaluated by any AVB, and both sides compete on quality and innovation. This is how the ecosystem scales.

## 2.3 Marketplace Principles
The ARAF ecosystem operates on marketplace principles designed to prevent monopolization and encourage quality competition:
Multiple AVBs: ARAF accredits multiple AVBs. No single firm holds exclusive evaluation rights. Certified organizations may choose any accredited AVB.
Multiple CAPOs: ARAF accredits multiple CAPOs. AVBs and certified organizations may choose any accredited CAPO. CAPOs compete on platform capability, reliability, and cost.
Multiple Insurers: ARAF recognizes multiple insurer partners. Certified organizations choose their insurer. Insurers compete on coverage terms and premium.
Interoperability: AVBs and CAPOs must implement the ARAF interoperability protocol, ensuring certified organizations are not locked into a single vendor pairing.
Portability: Certified organizations may switch AVBs or CAPOs without recertification, provided the new AVB/CAPO accepts the existing certification data and the transition follows the handoff protocol defined in Article 8.

# Article 3: Authorized Validation Bodies (AVBs)

## 3.1 Role Definition
Authorized Validation Bodies are third-party organizations accredited by ARAF to conduct evaluations of autonomous systems against the ARA Standard and to recommend certification decisions. AVBs are the “auditors” of the ARA ecosystem. They assess whether a system meets the requirements of its applicable ACRs at the requested certification level.
AVBs do NOT grant certification directly. AVBs conduct evaluations and submit certification recommendations to ARAF. ARAF issues the certification based on the AVB’s recommendation, the system’s CAPO telemetry (for Level 2 and Level 3), and any additional ARAF review.

Organizations that meet all requirements except the 2-year track record may apply for Provisional AVB status. ## 3.6 AVB Rights and Obligations

## 3.7 Annual Review and Renewal
AVB accreditation is reviewed annually. The review includes: evaluation quality assessment (random sample review of completed evaluations), evaluator competence verification, insurance and organizational status confirmation, complaint history review, and methodology conformance check. Accreditation renewal requires Accreditation Committee majority vote. Renewal is for a 12-month period.

## 3.8 Suspension and Revocation
AVB accreditation may be suspended for: failure to maintain accreditation requirements, quality deficiencies identified during annual review or complaint investigation, failure to submit evaluation reports timely, or material misrepresentation. Suspension is effective immediately upon notice. The AVB has 60 days to remediate. Failure to remediate results in revocation. Revoked AVBs may reapply after 12 months.

# Article 4: Continuous Assurance Platform Operators (CAPOs)

## 4.1 Role Definition
Continuous Assurance Platform Operators are technology companies accredited by ARAF to operate monitoring platforms that ingest telemetry from certified autonomous systems, verify ongoing compliance with ARA Standard requirements, and provide real-time risk signals to AVBs, insurers, and certified organizations.
CAPOs are the infrastructure layer that makes persistent certification possible. Without CAPOs, ARA certification would be a point-in-time assessment, no different from SOC 2 or ISO 27001. With CAPOs, certification becomes a living, continuously verified state.
CAPOs do NOT conduct evaluations or recommend certifications. That is the exclusive role of AVBs. CAPOs provide the continuous monitoring data that informs revalidation decisions, supports insurance underwriting, and gives certified organizations real-time visibility into their compliance posture.

# Article 5: Recognized Insurer Partners (RIPs)

## 5.1 Role Definition
Recognized Insurer Partners are insurance companies or syndicates that have been recognized by ARAF as qualified to underwrite autonomous system operational risk using ARA certification data and CAPO telemetry feeds. They provide insurance products that complement ARA certification, enabling the “ARA Certified + Insured” badge (ARAF-ARA-TMK-2026-002, Article 6.7).
Insurance integration is a strategic differentiator for the ARA ecosystem. By connecting certification data directly to underwriting, ARAF creates a feedback loop where certification quality directly affects insurance premiums, and insurance claims data informs standard improvements. This loop does not exist in any competing certification framework.

## 5.5 Data Access and Privacy
Insurer partners receive only aggregated risk signals, not raw telemetry. The Insurance Data Feed (ARAF-ARA-SDK-2026-002, Article 6.6) provides seven signal categories: Certification Health, Boundary Incidents, Drift Alerts, Availability, Privacy Incidents, Societal Safety, and Revalidation History. All data is scoped to the insured system only. Insurers may not request or receive data about uninsured systems. Data sharing requires a tripartite agreement between the certified organization, the CAPO, and the insurer.

## 5.6 Annual Review
Insurer partner recognition is reviewed annually. The review includes: regulatory standing verification, financial stability check, integration health assessment, product accuracy review (insurance products still accurately represent ARA certification), and claims data sharing compliance. Recognition renewal is administrative unless issues are identified.

# Article 8: Interoperability and Portability

## 8.1 Purpose
The ARAF Interoperability Protocol ensures that certified organizations are not locked into a single AVB or CAPO. Portability is a core marketplace principle: organizations must be able to switch providers without losing certification status, telemetry history, or compliance continuity.

## 8.2 AVB Portability
A certified organization may switch AVBs at any point during its certification period. The transition process is:
The organization notifies ARAF and the current AVB of its intent to switch.
The current AVB transfers the most recent evaluation report and evidence package to ARAF (not directly to the new AVB, to preserve independence).
The new AVB receives the evaluation report from ARAF and conducts a transition review (not a full re-evaluation) to confirm familiarity with the system.
The new AVB assumes responsibility for the next reassessment.
Certification status is unaffected during the transition.

## 8.3 CAPO Portability
A certified organization may switch CAPOs. The transition process is:
The organization notifies ARAF, the current CAPO, and the evaluating AVB.
The current CAPO exports the organization’s telemetry data in the ARAF Standard Export Format within 48 hours of request.
The new CAPO imports the telemetry data and establishes baseline monitoring.
A monitoring continuity gap of up to 72 hours is permitted during CAPO transition for Level 2. For Level 3, a monitoring gap of more than 4 hours requires ARAF notification and may trigger a revalidation review.
The current CAPO retains a copy of the exported data per its data retention obligations.

## 8.4 Standard Export Format
The ARAF Standard Export Format is a structured JSON archive containing: all telemetry events for the export period (minimum 13 months), all compliance check results and alert history, all configuration snapshots (SDK version, privacy modes, monitoring parameters), certification checkpoint data (check-in history, revalidation triggers), and a cryptographic integrity manifest enabling the receiving CAPO to verify data completeness and authenticity. All accredited CAPOs must support both import and export of the Standard Export Format.

## 8.5 Insurer Transition
Certified organizations may switch insurer partners at any time. Insurance transitions do not affect certification status. The outgoing insurer notifies ARAF of policy cancellation. The incoming insurer notifies ARAF of new coverage. The ARAF registry and tokenized badge update automatically. There is no insurance data portability requirement between insurers.

## 10.2 Governance Evolution
As the ecosystem grows, ARAF governance will evolve:
Board expansion: Board will expand to include elected representatives from AVBs, CAPOs, and certified organizations as each category reaches critical mass (5+ participants).
TSB elections: TSB membership will transition from appointed to partially elected, with consortium members voting for a portion of TSB seats.
Regional representation: Advisory councils will include geographic representation requirements to ensure global relevance.
Regulatory engagement: ARAF will establish formal liaison relationships with regulatory bodies (NIST, EU AI Office, UK AI Safety Institute, Singapore IMDA) as adoption grows.
ARAF maintains active awareness of complementary standards bodies operating in adjacent layers of the agentic AI stack. These include the Agentic AI Foundation (AAIF, Linux Foundation) governing interoperability protocols including MCP; the Coalition for Secure AI (CoSAI, OASIS Open Project) addressing AI security practices and secure-by-design agentic system principles; the UK DSIT AI Assurance Roadmap; and IEEE P2863 (Organizational Governance of Artificial Intelligence). The NIST Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) AI Agent Standards Initiative, announced February 2026, extends the AI Risk Management Framework into agent-specific territory; ARA certification is designed to operationalize and complement this emerging guidance. None of these bodies are competitive with ARAF—each addresses a distinct layer of the AI assurance landscape.

## 10.3 Standard Versioning and Backward Compatibility
ARAF commits to the following versioning principles:
Minor updates (v1.1 → v1.2) maintain backward compatibility. Certified organizations do not need immediate recertification.
Major updates (v1.x → v2.0) may break backward compatibility. Certified organizations receive a minimum 12-month transition period.
ACR additions are non-breaking: new ACRs do not affect existing certifications until reassessment.
ACR removals include a 6-month sunset period.
All versions are permanently archived and accessible through the ARAF document library.

Term | Definition
Accreditation | Formal recognition by ARAF that an AVB or CAPO meets the requirements to perform its designated role in the ARA ecosystem.
ARAF | Autonomous Reliability Assurance Foundation. The independent standards organization governing the ARA ecosystem.
ATAG | AI Safety and Adversarial Testing Advisory Group. Advisory council to the TSB.
AVB | Authorized Validation Body. A third-party organization accredited to conduct ARA evaluations.
CAP | Continuous Assurance Platform. The technology infrastructure that enables persistent certification through real-time telemetry monitoring.
CAPO | Continuous Assurance Platform Operator. A technology company accredited by ARAF to operate a CAP.
Certification | The formal recognition that an autonomous system meets ARA Standard requirements at a specified level.
Consortium Member | An organization that participates in the ARAF Founding Consortium at a specified tier.
DPSIC | Data Privacy and Societal Impact Council. Advisory council to the TSB.
Insurance Data Feed | The standardized data exchange between CAPOs and recognized insurers providing aggregated risk signals.
Interoperability Protocol | The ARAF-defined protocol enabling portability of certification data between AVBs and CAPOs.
Persistent Certification State | The ARA concept that certification is continuously verified through telemetry, not just assessed at a point in time.
RCIAC | Risk, Compliance, and Insurance Advisory Council. Advisory council to the TSB.
Recognition | Formal acknowledgment by ARAF that an insurer partner meets requirements to participate in the ecosystem.
RIP | Recognized Insurer Partner. An insurance company recognized by ARAF to underwrite ARA-related risk.
RPSC | Robotics and Physical Systems Council. Advisory council to the TSB.
Standard Export Format | The structured JSON archive format for portable telemetry and certification data between CAPOs.
System Profile | One of four capability-based classifications (Foundational, Standard, Advanced, Comprehensive) determining which ACRs apply.
Technical Contributor | An organization or individual contributing technical expertise and financial support to ARAF at the Technical Contributor tier.
TSB | Technical Standards Board. The ARAF body responsible for standard development, accreditation criteria, and technical governance.
Assurance Class | The second axis of an ARA Deployment Certification. Describes the intensity of ongoing monitoring required to maintain the certification in good standing: Class A Periodic, Class B Monitored, or Class C Continuously Assured. Determined by Risk Classification. Not applicable to Platform Certifications.
Assurance Lapse | The state in which a Deployment Certification’s CAPO monitoring has fallen below the frequency required by its Assurance Class. Triggers a class-dependent remediation window; if unresolved, results in Suspension or Expiry.
Certified Technology Partner (CTP) | A consulting or implementation firm listed in the ARAF CTP Registry as having demonstrated ARA implementation competency. Not a certification — a registry designation. Does not confer evaluation authority or certification rights.
Certification Inheritance | The mechanism by which a Deployment Certification built on a Platform-Certified product carries forward the platform’s ACR coverage, reducing the scope of deployment-level evaluation to configuration-dependent and deployment-context ACRs.
Class A — Periodic | Assurance Class requiring annual or biannual re-evaluation. No continuous CAPO monitoring required. Event-triggered revalidation only. Applicable to low-autonomy, low-consequence, reversible deployments.
Class B — Monitored | Assurance Class requiring an active CAPO connection and minimum monthly telemetry batch delivery. Event-triggered revalidation. Applicable to operational business systems with moderate autonomy or consequence.
Class C — Continuously Assured | Assurance Class requiring a persistent CAPO connection, real-time telemetry stream, and SLA-bound alerting. Required for high-autonomy, high-consequence, or regulated deployments. Life-safety systems are Class C without exception.
Deployment Certification | An ARA certification issued to a specific production instance of an autonomous system operated by an identified organization. Covers the full operational context including configuration, integrations, data environment, and use cases. Expressed as an Evaluation Level and an Assurance Class. Issued to the deploying organization.
Evaluation Level | The first axis of an ARA certification. Describes the rigor of the evaluation conducted by an accredited AVB: Level 1 Foundation, Level 2 Operational, or Level 3 Comprehensive. Applies to both Platform Certifications and Deployment Certifications.
Platform Certification | An ARA certification issued to a software platform, product, or autonomous system technology evaluated in a controlled reference environment against applicable ACRs. Certifies the technology as a product independent of any specific production deployment. Expressed as an Evaluation Level only (no Assurance Class). Issued to the technology vendor.
Reference Environment | A controlled deployment environment used for Platform Certification evaluation. Must meet ARAF minimum conditions for ACR evaluation fidelity per Art. 3.7 of the Evaluation Methodology Guide. Documented and attested by the evaluating AVB as representative of production-equivalent behavior.
Risk Classification | A formal assessment conducted by an accredited AVB as part of Deployment Certification that determines the required Assurance Class. Based on degree of autonomy, consequence severity, reversibility, breadth of impact, regulatory context, dependency criticality, and operational continuity. Documented in the Risk Classification Report. Appealable to the ARAF Technical Standards Board.
